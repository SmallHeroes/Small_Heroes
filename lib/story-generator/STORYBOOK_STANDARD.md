# Storybook Standard — Small Heroes

The literary quality bar for Small Heroes books.

A story can pass every structural, resilience, safety and technical-Hebrew
gate and still fail **this** bar — and if it does, it is not a sellable
children's book.

**The bar in one line:** it is not enough that the story is *correct*.
It must be a story a child *wants to come back to*.

This document is the single source of truth for two things:

1. **The Author prompt (generation)** — the "good" the Author writes toward.
2. **The Storybook Reviewer (measurement)** — the "good" the reviewer scores.

It owns **prose, voice and relationship** only. It never touches the
therapeutic arc, the resilience mechanic, page count, or safety — those are
owned by the Recipe and the validators.

---

## Hard guardrails

Anything built from this document — the Author, the Reviewer — must NEVER:

- turn a calm / therapeutic story into an adventure, or add peril / adrenaline
- add a moral or a lesson ("ומאז היא ידעה ש...")
- change the resilience arc, the companion mechanic, or the page structure
- make the companion speak, cast, or transform
- trade emotional truth for excitement

A Small Heroes book is quiet by design. "More action" is never the fix for
"flat." The fix for flat is **relationship and feeling** — not plot.

---

## Emotional is not poetic

Writing a *moment* does not mean writing literary or atmospheric prose. A
children's book is simple, plain and warm. A feeling reaches the reader
through a **body**, a real **object**, or physical **closeness** — never
through a poetic summary, and never through an abstract idea.

- Do not write "summary" sentences that step back and name the mood — show
  the moment, never label it.
- Do not make an abstract noun the subject of a sentence. שתיקה / רגע /
  פחד / מחשבה / נועם do not perform actions; the subject is the child, her
  body, the companion, or a real object.
- Similes compare to a simple real thing a child knows (a button, a
  blanket, a pillow) — never to an abstract or poetic image.
- The child's name appears exactly as given.

Examples:

- ✗ POETIC: "שתיקת שניהם מילאה את הרגע." → ✓ "נועה שמה יד ליד בּוֹלִי. שניהם נשארו רגע בשקט."
- ✗ ABSTRACT: "האור נראה כמו כתם רגוע." → ✓ "האור על הקיר היה רך, לא חזק בעיניים."
- ✗ ELEVATED: "נועם נשימתה השתנה." → ✓ "נועה נשמה קצת אחרת."

"Emotional, not poetic" is the calibration point: the failure mode on one
side is a dry action-report; on the other side is literary atmosphere.
Aim for the middle — simple, alive, close.

## The 8 dimensions

Each page, and the book as a whole, is judged on these. Score 1–5.

### 1. naturalHebrew
Correct, native, read-aloud Hebrew. A parent reads it aloud without
stumbling. No invented words, no translated syntax, no adult / cold words.

- BAD: "המדחום נראה כמו דגיק רגוע ונושם" — invented word
- BAD: "הרגע קופא ונמוג" — adult, abstract
- GOOD: "המדחום היה קר וקטן, והקצה שלו נצנץ"

### 2. storybookVoice
It reads as a *story*, not a storyboard. The dead giveaway is the
action-report rhythm: "X did A. Y did B. X did C."

- BAD: "דניאל התיישבה. בּוֹלִי התגלגל. דניאל הסתכלה. בּוֹלִי נפתח."
- GOOD: "טוּמְפּ קטן נשמע מהכיס. דניאל עצרה רגע — ואז הציצה פנימה."

### 3. relationshipAndEmotion — *the core*
The child and the companion respond to *each other*. Not in words — in the
body. Emotion causes the action; the companion's action answers the child's
feeling. The relationship **is** the story. Two characters doing things in
parallel is not a story.

- BAD: "דניאל ראתה את המדחום. בּוֹלִי התגלגל בכיס."
- GOOD: "דניאל ראתה את המדחום, והיד שלה נכנסה לכיס. משם, ממש ליד האצבעות,
  יצא טוּמְפּ קטן." — feeling → action → the companion answers

### 4. pageMoment
Each page holds ONE moment a child can picture — with a change, a feeling
or a connection inside it. A page that is technically fine but where
nothing shifts emotionally is a **dead page**.

- Ask: can a child draw this page as one picture? Is there one beat that
  *moves*?

### 5. sentenceVariety
Varied sentence and page openings. Not every page opening with the child's
name or with "בּוֹלִי". Not a run of subject–verb–object.

- RED FLAG: more than ~3 pages in the book opening with the child's name.
- Lead with sound, object, place or feeling: "מתחת לשמיכה...",
  "טוּמְפּ קטן...", "המדחום...".

### 6. childVocabulary
Every word is understood by a 5–6-year-old.

- BAD: "לוח" — technical; a child hears "blackboard". "נמוג"; "פרט".
- GOOD: the companion's shell → "פס שריון", "השריון"; small, warm,
  concrete words.

### 7. companionLiveliness
The companion is a *friend* — never an object, never a tool. He is never
"placed", "rested", or "stored". He peeks, curls up, leans close, answers
with a טוּמְפּ.

- BAD: "בּוֹלִי נח בכיס התרמיל."
- GOOD: "בּוֹלִי התכרבל בכיס התרמיל, חמים ושקט." / "מכיס התרמיל הציץ בּוֹלִי."

### 8. readAgainFactor
At least one small, concrete, sensory moment per book that a child will
remember and ask for again — and description that acts on the body or the
scene, not generic decoration.

- BAD: "אור לבן. ריח של סבון. קיר לבן."
- GOOD: "מיכל קימטה את האף מריח הסבון." / "המדבקה כמעט נדבקה לשריון של
  בּוֹלִי."

## Verdict scale (for the Reviewer)

- **PASS** — reads like a real children's book. Ship.
- **WEAK** — structurally fine, but flat / mechanical / thin on relationship.
  Needs a generation fix, not a patch.
- **FAIL** — bad Hebrew, dead pages, no relationship, pure action-report.

Every issue the Reviewer raises must cite an **exact quote** from the story,
name the **page**, and name which of the **8 dimensions** it violates.

---

## The two rules that carry most of the weight

1. **Do not write a chain of actions.** Every page must carry at least one
   of: a feeling in the child's body · the companion answering · a small
   change *between* the child and the companion · a concrete object that
   holds emotion.

2. **The companion is not an object and not a tool.** The companion
   responds to the child; the child responds back. That response *is* the
   story.
